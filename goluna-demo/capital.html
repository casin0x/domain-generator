01 The Question We Need to Align On

From M7 onward, every profitable month produces cash. That cash has two basic destinations: reinvested into the business to grow the next month’s profit, or distributed to investors as a return on their capital. The right answer is rarely “all of one.” What we need to agree on is the split, the milestones at which the split changes, and the conditions that must hold before any cash starts moving out.

The case for reinvestment

What retained earnings buy us

  • Bankroll growth. A larger bankroll lets the casino accept bigger players and run bigger races without taking on tail-risk.
  • Marketing scale. As long as CAC stays well below LTV, every dollar reinvested into acquisition returns more than a dollar paid out.
  • New verticals. Sportsbook expansion, live casino, poker, and additional product lines each require upfront capital.
  • Senior hires. Compliance, growth, and senior engineering become possible at meaningful Net Profit levels.
  • Optionality. Retained cash gives the business the ability to act fast when opportunities or threats appear.
The case for distribution

What investor payouts deliver

  • Capital recouped. Distributions return invested capital to the cap table, reducing time-to-payback risk.
  • Trust and momentum. Visible cash flow back to investors strengthens the relationship and supports any future raise.
  • Discipline. A scheduled distribution prevents retained earnings from sitting idle past the point where reinvestment still earns its cost of capital.
  • Alignment with expectations. Some investors structured their commitment expecting a dividend stream; under-distributing breaks that implicit agreement.
  • Personal liquidity. Investors may need or want cash returned for reasons unrelated to the business.

03 Proposed Distribution Activation Threshold

The four conditions below should all hold before any distribution begins. The intent is to make sure the first cheque to investors is paid from a business with proven repeatability, an insulated bankroll, a real cash buffer, and enough operating history that the result is not a one-month spike.

All four conditions must hold simultaneously. If the bankroll is funded but only two profitable months have occurred, distributions stay at zero. The threshold is conservative on purpose; relaxing any condition individually is a discussion to have with investors, not a unilateral decision.
04 Proposed Phased Distribution Ramp

Once the activation threshold is met, distribution as a percentage of after-pool Net Profit scales with the NP band. The ramp is intentionally gentle — investors see cash growing in step with the business, while the company retains enough to keep compounding into the next stage of growth.

06 The Diminishing Returns Signal

Reinvesting indefinitely is not the goal. Past a certain point, retained earnings stop earning their cost of capital and become idle balance-sheet cash. When the signals below start to appear, the bias should shift from “retain heavily” to “distribute more aggressively” — even if the NP band would otherwise suggest a lower payout percentage.

Signal What it means Suggested response
CAC : LTV ratio climbing toward 1 : 2Acquisition is getting more expensive relative to retention. The marketing-reinvestment thesis is weakening.Cap marketing spend at current efficient level, raise distribution % by one band.
Best available marketing channel ROAS < 2×There is no profitable channel left at current spend levels. Pouring more into acquisition would destroy capital.Reduce marketing budget, redirect surplus to distribution or higher-tier investments.
Cash balance > 12 months opex without a planned useReserve has grown beyond the resilience threshold and is now idle.Distribute the excess as a one-time payment alongside the regular distribution.
No vertical or hire passes ROI screen for two quartersThe reinvestment hierarchy has run out of tiers that earn their capital. Retention is hoarding, not investing.Move to the upper distribution band ahead of the NP-based ramp.
Investor signals a liquidity needAn investor needs cash returned for reasons outside the business. Hoarding to optimise growth becomes selfish in this case.Discuss a one-time distribution or accelerated ramp to address the need.
07 Proposed Review Cadence

This policy should be a living document, not a fire-and-forget contract. Two review cadences are proposed: a regular quarterly check with investors, and a set of mandatory review triggers that bring the policy back to the table outside the regular cadence.

Cadence What gets reviewed
Quarterly — standing reviewBankroll level, cash reserve, NP trend, marketing efficiency, hierarchy progress against targets, and whether the current NP band remains appropriate. Investors and operating team review together.
Mandatory trigger · bankroll change > 25%A material change in bankroll up or down brings the threshold and the hierarchy back to the table.
Mandatory trigger · NP drops 30%+ for 2 consecutive monthsSustained drop in profitability re-opens the conversation about whether the distribution band should pause or step down.
Mandatory trigger · new vertical or hire proposedA material new investment uses capital that would otherwise flow to distribution, and should be approved jointly.
Mandatory trigger · M&A or fundraising opportunityA strategic event changes the underlying assumptions of this policy. The whole framework should be re-examined.
Annual — full policy re-examinationOnce per year, the entire framework — hierarchy, thresholds, ramp, signals — is opened for revision in light of the previous year’s actual results.
08 Open Questions for Investor Discussion

The items below are decisions that should not be made without investor input. They are listed so that the discussion has a clear agenda and so that nothing important is decided by default in the absence of an explicit conversation.

Structure
Is the underlying model dividend or exit?
Some investors put cash in expecting an annual dividend stream; others expect to be cashed out at exit and prefer all profit reinvested in the meantime. The split percentages above assume a dividend-stream model. If the investor expectation is exit-oriented, the entire ramp may need to be shifted right — distributing less, building enterprise value instead.
Timing
Is M12 acceptable as the earliest distribution trigger, or should it be later?
M12 is the earliest the data supports a non-arbitrary distribution decision. Some investors prefer to wait until M18 or M24 for additional certainty. Pushing the trigger out increases the bankroll and growth capital available; bringing it in earlier returns capital faster but increases the risk of distributing from what later turns out to be an unstable profit base.
Capital structure
Are the bankroll floor ($300K) and target ($500K) the right numbers?
The bankroll figures shown are reasonable defaults for a crypto casino at this stage but should be calibrated to the specific player profile. If the expected max-bet size is materially higher than current expectations, both numbers should rise. If the casino plans to remain in a lower-bet segment, the targets could be lower.
Capital structure
Should there be a minimum-distribution floor once the threshold is met?
An alternative to a strict percentage-of-NP model is a fixed minimum distribution (for example, $10K per month per investor) once the threshold is met, with the variable percentage layered on top. This guarantees a baseline cash return regardless of NP volatility, at the cost of slightly less reinvestment flexibility.
Timing
What cadence: monthly, quarterly, or annual distribution?
Monthly distribution mirrors monthly NP volatility and gives the most direct feedback loop. Quarterly is administratively simpler and smooths out monthly noise. Annual is the simplest but creates a lump-sum experience that may not match what investors actually want.
Tax & structure
What is the distribution vehicle — dividend, director’s loan repayment, or capital return?
The mechanism matters for tax in every party’s jurisdiction. Dividend treatment, treatment as repayment of director’s loans, or capital return all have different consequences. This needs the input of accountants on each side before the first distribution is made.
Structure
Is the proposed review cadence and trigger list complete?
The list in §07 is a starting point. Investors may have specific events — macro changes, regulatory shifts, competitor actions — that they would like to add as mandatory review triggers. Better to enumerate them now than to debate them under pressure later.
Capital structure
Are future capital calls or fundraises in scope?
This policy assumes no further capital is being raised. If a future raise is possible — for an MGA license, a new vertical, or geographic expansion — the policy should specify whether distributions pause during the raise, whether existing investors get preference, and how the dilution math interacts with the distribution ramp.
