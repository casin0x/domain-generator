Growth Paths — A vs B

Two budgets, same 1-month onboarding + 3-month pilot (M2–M4), same M4 GO/KILL gate, same total spend.

  • Path A — acquisition volume. The standard crypto-casino playbook the rest of the dashboard models.
  • Path B — retention first. Built on one insight: cheap bonus-hunters cost more than expensive retained players.
  • Same spend, very different M7+ run-rate.
Currency: Default USD · FX 1 USD ≈ 10.5 SEK · 1 EUR ≈ $1.08.
01 The Strategic Question

The mentor’s lesson behind Path B, condensed:

“For a year we were obsessed with one number: cost per acquisition. Lower it, win the quarter. So we widened the funnel. Bigger bonuses, broader targeting, fewer signup steps. Signups doubled. The dashboard looked beautiful. Then I pulled the 30-day retention curve. Almost everyone we’d ‘won’ had vanished before a second deposit. We hadn’t acquired players. We’d rented bonus hunters.” Casino operator mentor · LinkedIn excerpt

Applied to GoLuna:

  • The channel mix leans bonus-hunter. BH Ads = 47% of M1–M6 marketing — structurally the bonus-hunter channel.
  • The LTV assumption is load-bearing. The Revenue P&L assumes ~$130 LTV per FTD (6 mo) — which needs a 2nd-deposit rate of 35–40%.
  • The realistic default is lower. Poorly-engineered onboarding runs 22–25%.
  • The gap decides the gate. If retention lands at the default instead of the engineered level, the M4-exit run-rate sits $10–18K below the ~$35K floor — that’s a kill, not a smaller profit.
  • Path B earns the LTV instead of hoping for it.
Now backed by real retention data.  The retention lead’s prior book gives actual retention rates: ~19% monthly retention and a week-4 cohort of ~8%. That is the base the model should assume, with 30% as the engineered target. Combined with Brazil-market ARPU and CPA, see the Model Assumptions → page for the full input set.

04 Structural Changes (No Direct Cash Cost)

Path B isn’t just a marketing reshuffle — it changes how bonuses, affiliates, and onboarding work. These structural changes have no direct cash cost but materially affect player quality. They’re the leverage that earns the LTV the Revenue model is already assuming.

Lever Path A (current) Path B (proposed)
Welcome bonus Standard 100%+ match-deposit cap, low wagering. Maximises signup — and signal to bonus hunters. 50% match cap, normal wagering (~25–30x). Filters out bonus hunters at the door. Players who deposit through this offer want the casino, not the freebie.
Reload structure Light reload bonuses, standard schedule. Aggressive weekly reload (e.g. 30% match every Monday) + rakeback program. Rewards stickiness. Money saved on welcome funds this.
Onboarding flow Implicit. Customer.io is paid for but no documented trigger flow. Most operators learn about new players days after signup. Designed and deployed in M1: welcome message immediately · 24h check-in · 48h personalised offer by game type if no 2nd deposit · 7-day reactivation if dormant. Built once, runs forever.
Affiliate commission Flat 30% on NGR for all affiliates. Rewards volume regardless of player quality. Tiered: 20% baseline + 5–10% retention bonus on D60+ NGR. Bonus-aggregator affiliates earn less; SEO/community affiliates earn more. Same blended cost when player quality is mixed, much lower cost when affiliate sends bonus hunters.
Tracked KPIs CAC, FTD count, GGR, NGR. All Path A KPIs plus: 2nd-deposit rate (target ≥35%), D30 retention (target ≥15%), LTV by channel, cohort revenue at week 2 / week 4 / week 8. Surfaces retention failure within 30 days, not 90.
Acquisition / retention org Acquisition (Gibbiana) and retention (Owl/Toro via Customer.io) are separate concerns, talking after campaigns run. Joint weekly review of cohort retention by channel. Acquisition channel decisions get made based on 2nd-deposit rate, not CPA. Forces channels with bad retention to either improve or close.
05 Pros & Cons

Honest framing: neither path is the right answer in the abstract. Path A is the established playbook with known dynamics. Path B is the better long-run shape if execution lands — but the execution risk is real, especially the M1 onboarding-flow build.

Path A

Acquisition-Volume Optimised

Pros
  • Predictable. BH Ads / clipping / paid social are well-understood channels with known unit economics. Lower execution risk in M1–M3.
  • Faster top-line ramp. Bigger welcome bonus + broader funnel produces more FTDs in M1–M3 — the metric most easily shown in early investor updates.
  • Standard playbook. Affiliates, processors, and bonus aggregators all expect this structure. Less friction in vendor relationships.
  • Acquisition team can execute alone. Doesn’t require building the onboarding flow on day one — CRM work can be deferred without immediate cost.
  • Lower coordination cost. No need to align acquisition + retention before launch. Each function runs independently.
Cons
  • Bonus-hunter exposure. 43% of marketing spend goes to BH Ads — the channel most likely to produce single-deposit players.
  • Implicit LTV assumption may not hold. Model assumes ~$130 LTV; industry default for non-engineered onboarding is closer to $50–100.
  • M7+ run-rate weaker. Cohorts don’t compound — you keep paying CPA for replacement players because retention bleeds.
  • Affiliate program rewards volume not quality. Bonus-aggregator affiliates earn the same as community-driven affiliates.
  • Retention failure is invisible until M4–M5. By the time the cohort curve breaks, you’ve already spent $80K on bonus hunters.
Path B

Retention-Engineered

Pros
  • Earns the LTV the model assumes. Lower welcome + community channels filter for committed players. The $130 LTV becomes plausible, not aspirational.
  • M7+ run-rate compounds. Higher 2nd-deposit and D30 retention mean M1–M6 cohorts stack on top of each other instead of bleeding out.
  • Stronger brand defensibility. Community-driven players are stickier and less price-sensitive when competitor bonuses come along.
  • Surfaces problems faster. Cohort retention metrics expose channel failure within 30 days, not 90. Faster reallocation.
  • Better M7+ unit economics. Acquisition cost per retained player drops because retention is engineered, not hoped for.
Cons
  • Slower M1–M3 ramp. Fewer FTDs in early months — if investors are tracking that specific metric, the dashboard looks worse for the first quarter.
  • Execution risk on the 48h CRM flow. If the flow isn’t deployed in M1, you get Path A’s spend with Path B’s lower volume — worst of both.
  • Community manager hire risk. Telegram/Discord work needs someone who actually understands crypto-casino culture. A bad hire wastes the $9K and delays the channel.
  • Streamer pipeline depends on partnerships. $5K/mo only buys mid-tier streamers; if those slots are taken or partnerships fall through, the budget under-delivers.
  • Affiliate-commission restructure is contractually heavier. Tiered commissions are harder to sell to affiliates than flat ones. Some volume affiliates will just decline.
  • Slightly higher cumulative spend. +$6K M1–M6 makes the already-tight M6 budget remaining ~$6K deeper into the red.

08M4 Break-Even — The Kill Line

Same kill-at-M4 logic as Path C: after the M2–M4 pilot, the monthly GGR run-rate has to cover the fixed run-rate or you shut it down. Both A and B carry the 9% affiliate (47.5% net contribution) and a heavier in-house run-rate ($16.7K/mo), so their floor sits above Anzil’s.

The floor (below this = kill): ~$35K GGR/mo → ~$16.7K net/mo — net contribution at 47.5% covers the $16.7K run-rate. That’s ~1,170 monthly-active depositors at $30 ARPU. The floor is higher than Path C’s ~$28K because A/B carry the 9% affiliate + a bigger in-house team.
